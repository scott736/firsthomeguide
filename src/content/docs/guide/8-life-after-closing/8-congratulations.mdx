## Your First-Week Checklist

The first week in your new home is exciting — and busy. Here are the essential tasks to take care of right away:

### Safety and Security (Day 1-2)
- [ ] **Change all the locks.** This is non-negotiable. You do not know who has copies of the existing keys — previous owners, their family, former cleaners, contractors, neighbours, or past tenants. A locksmith can re-key all the locks in your home for $200 to $500, or you can replace the lock cylinders yourself for $50 to $150.
- [ ] **Locate the main water shut-off valve.** Know where it is and how to turn it off. If a pipe bursts at 2:00 AM, you need to stop the water flow immediately. The shut-off is typically in the basement, near the front of the house, or near the water meter.
- [ ] **Locate the electrical panel.** Know where your circuit breaker panel is and how to reset a tripped breaker. Label each breaker with the area or appliance it controls if the previous owner did not already do so.
- [ ] **Locate the gas shut-off valve** (if applicable). If your home has natural gas, know where the main gas shut-off is located (usually near the gas meter on the exterior of the house) and how to turn it off in an emergency.
- [ ] **Locate the furnace and water heater.** Know where they are, how old they are (check the labels), and how to turn them off if needed.
- [ ] **Test all smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors.** Press the test button on each unit. Replace batteries if they are old, and replace the entire unit if it is more than 10 years old. Check that there is at least one smoke detector on every level of the home and near all sleeping areas.

### Utilities and Services (Day 1-3)
- [ ] **Confirm all utilities are active** in your name — electricity, gas, water, sewer, internet, and any other services.
- [ ] **Set up garbage and recycling collection.** Contact your municipality to confirm your pickup schedule and any setup steps for a new account.
- [ ] **Set up mail forwarding** with Canada Post. The service costs approximately $100 to $150 for 12 months and redirects mail from your old address to your new one. This catches any mail you forgot to update.
- [ ] **Record all utility meter readings.** Take photos of your electricity, gas, and water meters on move-in day so you have a baseline and can verify that your first bills are accurate.

### Administrative Updates (First Week)
- [ ] **Update your address with the CRA.** This is important for receiving tax credits, benefits (like the GST/HST credit and Canada Child Benefit), and correspondence. You can update your address through your My Account portal on the CRA website.
- [ ] **Update your driver's licence and health card.** Most provinces require you to update these within a certain number of days of moving.
- [ ] **Update your address with your bank, credit cards, and insurance companies.**
- [ ] **Update your address with your employer** for payroll and tax purposes.
- [ ] **Notify your auto insurance company.** Your address affects your auto insurance premiums — a change of address could increase or decrease your rate.

### Getting Settled (First Week)
- [ ] **Do a thorough walkthrough** now that the home is empty (or mostly empty). Check every room, closet, outlet, faucet, and light switch. Note anything that needs attention and prioritize repairs.
- [ ] **Clean before unpacking.** This is the easiest time to deep clean — before furniture and boxes are everywhere. Focus on kitchens, bathrooms, and floors.
- [ ] **Meet your neighbours.** Introduce yourself. Good neighbour relationships make homeownership more enjoyable and can be invaluable when you need a recommendation for a plumber, advice on the neighbourhood, or someone to keep an eye on your house while you are away.
- [ ] **Take photos of every room.** Document the current condition of the home for your records. These photos can be useful for insurance purposes and for tracking improvements over time.

:::tip
You do not need to do everything in the first week. Focus on safety (locks, shut-offs, detectors), essential utilities, and the most critical administrative updates. Everything else — decorating, organizing, and non-urgent repairs — can wait. Give yourself permission to settle in gradually.
:::

## Your Financial First Steps

In addition to the practical tasks above, take care of these financial items in your first few months:

- **Set up your emergency fund contributions.** Even a small automatic transfer of $100 to $200 per payday into a dedicated high-interest savings account gets you started. You can increase it as your budget stabilizes.
- **File for the Home Buyers' Tax Credit.** On your next tax return, claim the Home Buyers' Tax Credit — a $10,000 non-refundable tax credit worth up to $1,500 in tax savings. If you purchased jointly, you and your partner can split the credit.
- **Set up your HBP repayment plan.** If you used the Home Buyers' Plan to withdraw from your RRSP for your down payment, repayments begin in the second year after withdrawal and are spread over 15 years. Set up a reminder so you do not miss the deadline — missed repayments are added to your taxable income.
- **Close or convert your FHSA.** If you used a First Home Savings Account, ensure the account is properly closed or converted after your qualifying purchase, per the program rules.
- **Review your home insurance policy.** Now that you are living in the home, confirm that your coverage reflects the actual contents and any features you want protected. Add sewer backup and overland flooding riders if you have not already.

## Keep This Guide Handy

Homeownership is a journey, not a destination. The house you bought today will need care, attention, and smart financial decisions for years to come. Here are situations where you will want to revisit this guide:

- **Mortgage renewal time** — Review the Mortgage Renewal Strategy section 120 days before your term ends. Do not auto-renew.
- **Major renovation planning** — Review the Energy Efficiency section for available grants and rebates before starting a project.
- **Tax time** — Review the government programs section to make sure you are claiming every credit and benefit you are entitled to.
- **Selling and buying again** — If you decide to move, much of this guide applies to your next purchase. And as a second-time buyer, some programs (like first-time buyer rebates) will no longer be available — making the information here even more valuable for planning.

:::tip
Bookmark FirstHomeGuide.ca and come back whenever you need a refresher — whether it is mortgage renewal time, you are planning a renovation, or you want to revisit the government programs available to you. This guide will be here whenever you need it.
:::

## A Few Final Words

Homeownership is a journey that comes with responsibilities, surprises, and rewards in equal measure. The house you bought today will be the backdrop for years of memories — celebrations, quiet evenings, milestones, and everyday moments that make a house a home.

Here are a few pieces of advice to carry with you:

- **Keep learning.** Tax rules, government programs, and mortgage products change regularly. Stay informed by checking back on this guide for updates and following trusted Canadian personal finance resources.
- **Build your team.** A good mortgage broker, real estate lawyer, accountant, and home inspector are professionals you will need throughout your homeownership journey — not just at purchase time. Keep their contact information handy.
- **Be patient with yourself.** Every homeowner makes mistakes. The key is to learn from them and make better decisions next time. Your first home does not need to be perfect — it just needs to be yours.
- **Enjoy your home.** Beyond all the financial planning and maintenance checklists, your home is where life happens. Make it yours. Make it comfortable. Make it a place you love coming back to.

Welcome home.
