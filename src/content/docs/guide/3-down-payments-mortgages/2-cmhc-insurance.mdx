If your [down payment is less than 20%](/guide/3-down-payments-mortgages/1-down-payment-rules/) of the purchase price, your mortgage is classified as a <GlossaryTooltip client:visible term="high-ratio mortgage" definition="A mortgage where the down payment is less than 20%, requiring mortgage default insurance (CMHC, Sagen, or Canada Guaranty)." />, and Canadian law requires you to purchase mortgage default insurance. This is one of the most significant hidden costs of buying a home with a small down payment, and it is essential that you understand how it works before you commit to a purchase price.

---

## What Is Mortgage Default Insurance?

[Mortgage default insurance](https://www.cmhc-schl.gc.ca/consumers/home-buying/mortgage-loan-insurance) protects the **lender** — not you — in case you stop making your mortgage payments and default on the loan. Despite the fact that it protects the lender, **you** are the one who pays the premium. This is a federal legal requirement, not something your lender decided to charge you.

The rationale behind this system is straightforward: lenders are taking on more risk when borrowers put down less than 20%. The insurance reduces the lender's risk, which in turn allows them to offer mortgages to buyers who might not otherwise qualify. Without this system, most first-time buyers would need a full 20% down payment to get a mortgage at all.

The insurance premium is a one-time fee, but it is substantial. On a typical first-time buyer purchase, it can range from roughly **$8,000 to over $30,000** depending on your purchase price and down payment amount.

---

## The Three Mortgage Insurers in Canada

There are three companies authorized to provide mortgage default insurance in Canada:

1. **<GlossaryTooltip client:visible term="CMHC" definition="Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation — the federal agency that provides mortgage default insurance required when your down payment is less than 20%." /> ([Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation](https://www.cmhc-schl.gc.ca/consumers/home-buying/mortgage-loan-insurance))** — A federal Crown corporation and the largest mortgage insurer. CMHC is the most well-known and often used as a shorthand for all mortgage insurance in Canada, even when another insurer is involved.

2. **Sagen (formerly Genworth Canada)** — A private-sector insurer that provides the same coverage as CMHC. Sagen was known as Genworth Canada until 2021.

3. **Canada Guaranty** — Another private-sector insurer offering mortgage default insurance to Canadian lenders.

Your lender chooses which insurer to use — you do not get to pick. The good news is that premium rates are virtually identical across all three providers, so the choice of insurer does not meaningfully affect your costs. Each insurer has slightly different underwriting guidelines for edge cases, but for most straightforward first-time buyer purchases, the experience is the same regardless of which insurer your lender uses.

## Premium Rates

The insurance <GlossaryTooltip client:visible term="premium" definition="The cost of mortgage default insurance, ranging from 2.8% to 4.0% of the mortgage amount, typically added to the mortgage balance." /> is calculated as a **percentage of the mortgage amount** (not the purchase price). The rate depends on the size of your down payment relative to the purchase price (see [CMHC's premium rate schedule](https://www.cmhc-schl.gc.ca/consumers/home-buying/mortgage-loan-insurance/mortgage-loan-insurance-premiums)):

| Down Payment Percentage | Insurance Premium (% of Mortgage Amount) |
|------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| 5.00% to 9.99% | **4.00%** |
| 10.00% to 14.99% | **3.10%** |
| 15.00% to 19.99% | **2.80%** |
| 20.00% or more | **Not required** |

Notice the significant jump between the 5% tier (4.00% premium) and the 10% tier (3.10% premium). This 0.9 percentage point difference can translate to thousands of dollars in savings, making the stretch from 5% to 10% down one of the most cost-effective moves a first-time buyer can make.

:::tip
If you are close to the 10% or 15% down payment thresholds, it is worth exploring whether you can reach the next tier. Moving from 5% to 10% down on a $500,000 home reduces your insurance premium from $19,000 to approximately $13,950 — a savings of over $5,000, plus you will also pay less interest on a smaller mortgage principal.
:::

## How the Premium Is Added to Your Mortgage

In most cases, the mortgage insurance premium is **added directly to your mortgage principal**. You do not need to write a cheque for it at closing. Instead, your mortgage balance increases by the amount of the premium, and you pay it off gradually along with the rest of your mortgage.

Here is a detailed example to show exactly how this works:

### Worked Example: $500,000 Home with 5% Down

- Purchase price: $500,000
- Down payment: $500,000 x 5% = $25,000
- Base mortgage amount: $500,000 - $25,000 = $475,000
- Insurance premium: $475,000 x 4.00% = **$19,000**
- Total mortgage (including insurance): $475,000 + $19,000 = **$494,000**

You are now paying interest on $494,000 instead of $475,000. At a 5% interest rate over a [25-year amortization](/guide/3-down-payments-mortgages/4-amortization/), that $19,000 premium actually costs you approximately **$33,000** in total when you include the interest charged on the premium itself over the life of the mortgage.

### Worked Example: $500,000 Home with 10% Down

- Purchase price: $500,000
- Down payment: $500,000 x 10% = $50,000
- Base mortgage amount: $500,000 - $50,000 = $450,000
- Insurance premium: $450,000 x 3.10% = **$13,950**
- Total mortgage (including insurance): $450,000 + $13,950 = **$463,950**

By doubling your down payment from $25,000 to $50,000, you reduced your insurance premium by over $5,000 and your total mortgage by more than $30,000.

### Worked Example: $500,000 Home with 20% Down

- Purchase price: $500,000
- Down payment: $500,000 x 20% = $100,000
- Base mortgage amount: $500,000 - $100,000 = $400,000
- Insurance premium: **$0**
- Total mortgage: **$400,000**

No insurance is required, and your mortgage is $94,000 lower than the 5% down scenario. The trade-off is that you needed $100,000 in savings instead of $25,000.

:::caution
Mortgage insurance is a significant cost that is easy to overlook because it gets rolled into your mortgage. On a $500,000 home with 5% down, you are paying roughly $33,000 in insurance premiums and interest over the life of the mortgage. This is not a fee you can negotiate or avoid if your down payment is under 20% — it is a legal requirement. Factor it into your total cost of homeownership when deciding how much home you can afford.
:::

## Provincial Sales Tax on Insurance Premiums

In some provinces, you must also pay **provincial sales tax (PST) on the mortgage insurance premium**, and this amount is due at closing — it cannot be added to your mortgage. This catches many first-time buyers off guard.

The provinces that charge PST on mortgage insurance premiums include Ontario (8%), Quebec (9.975%), and Saskatchewan (6%). In Ontario, for example, a $19,000 insurance premium would trigger $1,520 in PST that you must pay out of pocket at closing.

Make sure you budget for this additional cost when calculating your [closing expenses](/guide/7-closing-the-deal/1-closing-costs-overview/).

## Can You Avoid Mortgage Insurance?

The only way to avoid mortgage insurance entirely is to make a down payment of **20% or more**. There is no negotiation, no exemption, and no alternative. If your down payment is less than 20%, insurance is mandatory.

Some buyers explore creative strategies like receiving a larger gift from family, withdrawing from both an FHSA and RRSP under the HBP, or combining multiple savings sources to reach the 20% threshold. If you are close to 20%, it is worth doing the math to see whether the effort of saving the additional amount is worth the insurance premium you would avoid.

However, waiting to save 20% down is not always the right call. In a rising market, the home price appreciation you miss while saving could outweigh the insurance cost. This is a calculation that depends on your personal circumstances, your local market, and how quickly you can save.

## Recent Rule Changes (2024-2026)

Two important changes to the insured mortgage system took effect in **December 2024** and continue to apply in 2026:

- **Insured-mortgage cap raised to $1.5 million.** Before December 15, 2024, mortgage default insurance was only available on homes priced under $1 million. Today, you can get an insured mortgage on homes priced up to $1,499,999 — opening up high-ratio purchases to a wider range of urban homes.
- **30-year amortization for first-time buyers and new builds.** All first-time buyers (and any buyer of a newly built home) can now choose a 30-year amortization on an insured mortgage. The longer amortization adds approximately a **20 basis point premium surcharge** on top of the standard CMHC premium rate. Lower monthly payments are the upside; higher total interest paid over the life of the mortgage is the downside.

The federal **First-Time Home Buyers' GST/HST Rebate** received Royal Assent on March 12, 2026 and the CRA began accepting applications on March 17, 2026. It is separate from the CMHC system but worth knowing about: eligible first-time buyers of new-build homes can recover up to $50,000 in GST (or the federal portion of HST) on homes priced up to $1 million, with a phase-out to $1.5 million.
