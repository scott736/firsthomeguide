British Columbia offers some of the most valuable provincial programs for first-time home buyers in Canada. The Property Transfer Tax (PTT) exemption alone can save you up to $8,000, and as of April 2024, the thresholds were significantly increased to cover homes up to $835,000 — making the program relevant to the majority of BC first-time buyers. Additional programs provide ongoing benefits after you purchase. However, BC also has some of the highest home prices in the country, so understanding these programs and their thresholds is essential for maximizing your savings.

---

## [BC Property Transfer Tax (PTT)](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/gov/content/taxes/property-taxes/property-transfer-tax) Exemption

The Property Transfer Tax is BC's equivalent of a [land transfer tax](/guide/7-closing-the-deal/3-land-transfer-tax/). Normally, every buyer pays PTT based on a tiered rate structure:

| Fair Market Value Portion | PTT Rate |
|--------------------------|----------|
| First $200,000 | 1% |
| $200,001 to $2,000,000 | 2% |
| Over $2,000,000 | 3% |
| Over $3,000,000 (residential) | 5% |

On a $500,000 home, the PTT would be $8,000. On a $750,000 home, it would be $13,000.

### [First-Time Home Buyer PTT Exemption](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/gov/content/taxes/property-taxes/property-transfer-tax/exemptions/first-time-home-buyers)

First-time buyers in BC can qualify for an exemption from the Property Transfer Tax on **the first $500,000 of the purchase price**, saving up to **$8,000**. As of April 1, 2024, the program was significantly expanded: homes with a fair market value up to **$835,000** now qualify for the full exemption. A **partial exemption** is available for homes valued between $835,000 and $860,000, with the exemption gradually phased out. These thresholds were increased from the previous $500,000/$525,000 limits.

For homes above $860,000, the first-time buyer PTT exemption is not available, and you pay the full tax.

:::tip
**This is a major 2024 improvement.** Before April 1, 2024, only homes priced up to $500,000 qualified — shutting out most buyers in the Lower Mainland and Victoria. The new $835,000 threshold brings the program within reach of most BC first-time buyers.
:::

### How the Exemption Amount Is Calculated

The exemption applies to **the first $500,000 of the purchase price** regardless of the total purchase price. The PTT you save is the tax that would have applied to that first $500,000:

- 1% on the first $200,000 = $2,000
- 2% on $200,001–$500,000 = $6,000
- **Total maximum savings: $8,000**

You still pay PTT on any amount above $500,000 at the applicable rates.

### Eligibility Requirements

To qualify for the first-time buyer PTT exemption, you must meet all of the following criteria:

- You must be a **Canadian citizen or permanent resident**
- You must have **lived in BC for at least 1 year** immediately before the purchase date, OR have filed **2 BC income tax returns** within the 6 years before the purchase date
- You must be a [**first-time home buyer**](/guide/1-are-you-ready/1-first-time-buyer/) — you have never owned a principal residence anywhere in the world at any time
- The home must become your **principal residence**
- You must **move in within 92 days** and live there for at least one year
- The property must be **0.5 hectares (1.24 acres) or smaller**
- The fair market value of the property must be **$860,000 or less** (full exemption up to $835,000; partial exemption $835,000–$860,000)

### Worked Examples

**Example 1: $650,000 condo in Burnaby**
- PTT without exemption: $11,000
- First-time buyer exemption: applies to first $500K (saves $8,000)
- PTT on $500,001–$650,000: 2% × $150,000 = $3,000
- **You pay: $3,000 in PTT** (savings of $8,000)

**Example 2: $510,000 townhouse in Langley**
- PTT without exemption: $8,200
- First-time buyer exemption: applies to first $500K (saves $8,000)
- PTT on remaining $10,000: $200
- **You pay: $200 in PTT** (savings of $8,000)

**Example 3: $700,000 home in Surrey**
- PTT without exemption: $12,000
- First-time buyer exemption: applies to first $500K (saves $8,000)
- PTT on $500,001–$700,000: 2% × $200,000 = $4,000
- **You pay: $4,000 in PTT** (savings of $8,000)

**Example 4: $845,000 townhouse (partial exemption zone)**
- PTT without exemption: $14,900
- Partial exemption applies (between $835K and $860K threshold)
- **You pay: a reduced PTT amount** (partial exemption proportionally phased out)

**Example 5: $900,000 home in Vancouver**
- PTT without exemption: $16,000
- First-time buyer exemption: **not available** (above $860,000 threshold)
- **You pay: $16,000 in PTT**

:::tip
If you are looking at homes priced near the $835,000 to $860,000 range, it may be worth negotiating the purchase price down to stay within the full exemption threshold. The savings are worth up to $8,000. Factor this into your offer strategy.
:::

## Newly Built Home PTT Exemption

Separately from the first-time buyer exemption, BC offers a PTT exemption for **newly built homes** valued up to **$1,100,000** (as of April 1, 2024), with a partial exemption for homes between $1,100,000 and $1,150,000. These thresholds were increased from the previous $750,000/$800,000 limits.

### Key Details

- This exemption is available to **all buyers**, not just first-time buyers
- The home must be a **newly built** residence (new construction or converted from non-residential use)
- You must use the home as your **principal residence**
- The exemption can **save up to $20,000** on a $1,100,000 new build

:::tip
**Another major 2024 improvement.** Before April 1, 2024, the newly built home exemption only applied to homes up to $750,000. The new $1,100,000 threshold dramatically expands eligibility in Metro Vancouver, Victoria, and other high-cost markets where most new condos and townhouses are priced above the old limit.
:::

### Can You Combine Both Exemptions?

You cannot claim both the first-time buyer exemption and the newly built home exemption on the same purchase. The system applies whichever exemption provides you the greater benefit. In practice:

- **New build priced under $835,000 and you are a first-time buyer:** The newly built home exemption is generally broader (covers the full purchase price up to $1.1M), so it typically provides the larger benefit.
- **New build priced $835,000–$1,100,000 and you are a first-time buyer:** The first-time buyer exemption no longer applies (above $860K threshold), but the newly built home exemption still applies — saving you thousands.
- **New build priced over $1,100,000:** Neither exemption applies.

## BC Home Owner Grant

Once you own your home and move in, you can claim the **BC Home Owner Grant** every year. This grant reduces your annual property taxes.

### Grant Amounts

| Category | Annual Grant |
|----------|-------------|
| Standard Home Owner Grant | Up to **$570** per year |
| Northern and Rural Areas | Up to **$770** per year |
| Seniors, Veterans, Persons with Disabilities | **Additional $275** per year |

### Eligibility

- You must be a **Canadian citizen or permanent resident**
- The home must be your **principal residence**
- The home's assessed value must be under **$2,075,000** (2026 threshold — this is adjusted annually based on property assessments; it was $2,175,000 in 2025)
- For homes assessed between $2,075,000 and $2,189,000, a partial grant is available (reduced by $5 for every $1,000 over the threshold); above $2,189,000 the grant is $0

### How to Claim

You claim the Home Owner Grant each year through your municipality or the provincial government (depending on your location). Some municipalities include a claim form with your annual property tax notice. You can also claim online through the BC government website.

The grant is **not automatic** — you must actively claim it each year. If you forget, you lose that year's benefit.

:::caution
The BC Home Owner Grant must be claimed every year. It is not applied automatically when you purchase a home. Many new homeowners forget to claim it in their first year. Set an annual reminder when your property tax notice arrives (typically in late spring or early summer) to submit your grant application.
:::

## BC Foreign Buyer Tax Exemptions

While not specifically a first-time buyer program, it is worth noting that BC charges a **20% additional Property Transfer Tax** on property purchases by foreign nationals and foreign-controlled corporations in designated areas (Metro Vancouver, Fraser Valley, Nanaimo, Victoria, and Kelowna). Canadian citizens and permanent residents are exempt from this tax. If you are a Canadian citizen or permanent resident, you do not need to worry about this additional cost.

## Calculating Your Total BC Benefits

Here is an example of what a first-time buyer in BC could save by combining available programs:

**Scenario: First-time buyer purchasing a $750,000 condo in Metro Vancouver**

| Program | Savings |
|---------|---------|
| BC First-Time Buyer PTT Exemption (on first $500K of price) | $8,000 |
| Federal [Home Buyers' Tax Credit](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/topics/about-your-tax-return/tax-return/completing-a-tax-return/deductions-credits-expenses/line-31270-home-buyers-amount.html) (HBTC) | $1,500 |
| FHSA tax deductions (5 years at $8,000, 30% rate) | $12,000 |
| BC Home Owner Grant (first year) | $570 |
| **Total first-year savings** | **$22,070** |

With the 2024 threshold increase, most first-time buyers in Metro Vancouver now qualify for the full $8,000 PTT exemption. Before April 2024, a $750,000 purchase would have received **no PTT exemption at all**. And the Home Owner Grant continues every year — over 10 years, that adds another $5,700 to your total benefit. For a side-by-side view of how BC compares to other provinces, see the [program comparison table](/guide/4-government-programs/7-program-comparison/).
